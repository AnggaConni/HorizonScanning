Mangrove Action Lab & Coastal Challenge: Synergy for a Sustainable Future

SMA Negeri 8 Balikpapan & Initiator Team

May 01, 2026

Indonesia’s coastal regions play a highly strategic role in maintaining the balance of marine and terrestrial ecosystems. However, in recent decades, these areas have faced massive environmental pressures—from increasing marine debris and declining mangrove cover to the undeniable impacts of climate change felt by coastal communities. These conditions degrade environmental quality and severely impact the socio-economic sustainability of communities reliant on coastal resources.

The Blue Carbon Engine: Why Mangroves?

Mangroves have profound ecological functions for coastal environments. They are not merely natural shields against abrasion; they are the ultimate carbon sink engines.

  • Climate Mitigation (Blue Carbon):Mangroves are renowned as ecosystems capable of absorbing and storing massive amounts of carbon, contributing significantly to climate change mitigation efforts.
  • Critical Endemic Habitat:In Balikpapan Bay, the Sonneratia alba (perepat) mangrove vegetation is the absolute primary food source for Kalimantan's endemic primate, the Proboscis Monkey (Nasalis larvatus). The preservation of mangroves is a non-negotiable factor for their population sustainability (Lhota, 2010).

Phase I: The Professional Tools & Interventions

1. Background

The crisis described above is vividly unfolding in the Balikpapan Bay area, recognized as one of East Kalimantan's most vital mangrove ecosystems. Numerous studies indicate that this area is under severe stress due to land-use changes and coastal development activities. Satellite imagery-based research reveals a significant decline in mangrove coverage over recent years, making protection and rehabilitation efforts critically urgent to maintain coastal balance (Anwar et al., 2024).

Amidst these environmental challenges, the involvement of the younger generation becomes a pivotal element in coastal preservation. Environmental education that provides direct field experience cultivates deeper understanding, empathy, and responsibility toward sustainability. Through participatory, action-based learning, students do not merely study environmental concepts theoretically; they directly comprehend coastal dynamics and the challenges faced.

As part of the effort to strengthen environmental literacy within the educational sphere, SMA Negeri 8 Balikpapan has initiated a collaborative program entitled Mangrove Action Lab And Coastal Challenge, themed “Youth Action for a Sustainable Coast: A Collaboration Between Schools and TELKOM Indonesia in Hands-on Learning, Environmental Literacy, Digital Innovation, and Youth Leadership.” This program emphasizes active student involvement through field activities such as coastal cleanups, mangrove planting, direct ecosystem learning, and digital environmental campaigns.

Through the Corporate Social Responsibility (CSR) support from TELKOM Indonesia, this initiative aims to deliver a tangible contribution to coastal ecosystem preservation while empowering the youth to become integral parts of the sustainability movement.

2. Program Objectives

This program is structurally designed to achieve the following strategic targets:

  • A. Engage the younger generation in concrete actions to protect the coastal environment.
  • B. Support mangrove ecosystem rehabilitation through tree-planting activities.
  • C. Enhance student understanding of coastal ecosystems and blue carbon.
  • D. Develop environmental stewardship through field-based experiential learning.
  • E. Strengthen students' digital literacy via social media-driven environmental campaigns.
  • F. Build collaboration among schools, communities, and TELKOM Indonesia.

3. Core Activities & Technological Integration

To ensure that the environmental impact is accurately measured and scientifically structured, this program integrates two advanced EdTech and Environmental Design applications into the students' workflow.

Tech Integration 1: PesisirKita

PesisirKita is an offline-first EdTech application that empowers the younger generation to conduct geospatial mapping and ecological coastal recording like professional researchers. With just one click, all field data is automatically compiled into an elegant PDF portfolio that visualizes conservation impact through easily understandable data literacy.

Launch PesisirKita App

Tech Integration 2: The Atlas of Atmosphere

An evidence-based environmental design web application used during the Hackathon phase. It provides scientifically backed recommendations for optimizing room lighting, temperature, color palettes, and sensory elements tailored to specific activities. Its core function is to translate peer-reviewed psychological and physiological research into actionable "atmosphere recipes" to improve human performance and well-being.

Launch Atmosphere Atlas

The program will be executed through five primary activities:

A. Coastal Cleanup

Involving students, teachers, volunteers, and TELKOM employees. Activities include debris collection, waste segregation, and rudimentary data logging.

B. Mangrove Planting

Planting 500-1,000 seedlings for habitat restoration. Educating participants on Proboscis Monkey fodder species and the Blue Carbon concept.

C. Mangrove Action Lab

Field practice using PesisirKita. Students observe soil/water conditions, train in teamwork, and map geospatial environmental data directly.

D. Social Media Campaign

Students are trained to convey positive environmental messages via digital media under the theme: "Youth Action for a Sustainable Coast."

E. Young Environmental Hackathon

A creative space for students to design simple tech-based solutions for coastal problems. Using the Atmosphere Atlas, groups will develop innovative ideas, honing critical thinking, creativity, and problem-solving skills against environmental challenges.

Phase II: The Human Algorithm (When Technology Fails)

While PesisirKita and Atmosphere Atlas provide enterprise-grade capabilities for spatial mapping and environmental design, we must confront a harsh reality: What happens when technology fails in the field?

What happens when you are waist-deep in the mud of Balikpapan Bay, the cellular signal drops to zero, the equatorial sun renders your smartphone screen unreadable, or a sudden downpour shorts out your devices? If the student's capacity to analyze the environment is entirely dependent on an app, the mission collapses.

The ultimate technology is the human brain. To ensure true resilience, this program does not just teach students how to use apps. It trains them to internalize the software's logic. We train them to adopt an Integrated Horizon Scan Mindset and cultivate a Bayesian Brain.

The Bayesian Ecology Matrix

In machine learning (like Naive Bayes), every signal has a "confidence weight." We train students to think like the algorithm. When assessing an ecosystem without an app, they must stop using binary thinking (Good vs. Bad) and start thinking in probabilities based on weak signals.

Step 1: The Prior
Initial Belief

Before entering the sector, the student assumes: "This mangrove area looks untouched and healthy."

Probability of Health: 80%

Step 2: The Evidence
Signal Detection

Signal A: Absence of fiddler crabs (Medium Confidence signal of disturbance).
Signal B: Smell of H2S/Sulfur in the mud (High Confidence signal of anaerobic decay).

Step 3: The Posterior
Updated Calculation

The student's brain instantly calculates the weights. The sulfur smell drastically overrides the visual aesthetic.

Updated Health Prob: 30%

Training the Mindset: The 3-Step Protocol

How do we translate this high-level cognitive foresight into a trainable skill for high school students during the Mangrove Action Lab? We use a three-step cognitive integration protocol.

1. Sensory Calibration

Students are trained in exteroception (precision observation). Before pulling out a camera, they must stand still for 60 seconds. They calibrate their biological sensors to detect anomalies: the color gradient of the water, the specific density of the mud, or the unnatural silence of local birds. They become the hardware.

2. Probabilistic Weighting

Students are taught to abandon absolute certainty. When they see plastic waste, they don't just say "it's dirty." They apply Bayesian weighting: "This is a micro-plastic wrapper, sun-bleached. High probability it drifted from the city center, not dumped locally." They learn to assign confidence levels to field evidence.

3. The Mental Atlas

Just as the Atmosphere Atlas app prescribes lighting and temperature for productivity, students learn to "feel" the spatial environment naturally. They assess humidity, wind direction, and canopy shadow cover manually to deduce the micro-climate health of the mangrove sector before opening the app to verify it.

By embedding this Horizon Scanning and Bayesian mindset, we are not merely creating students who know how to use conservation apps. We are architecting a generation of critical thinkers whose strategic foresight functions independently of battery life. They become the ultimate environmental sensors.

7. Conclusion

The Mangrove Action Lab and Coastal Challenge is poised to become a concrete collaborative space merging the education sector, local communities, and corporate enterprises in safeguarding coastal sustainability. Partnering with TELKOM Indonesia ensures this initiative goes beyond mere physical action; it fundamentally nurtures environmental stewardship, cognitive resilience, and leadership among the youth.

Engaging students directly in these activities builds a powerful new awareness that protecting nature is a shared mandate. TELKOM Indonesia's support powerfully illustrates the corporation's commitment to advancing ESG (Environmental, Social, and Governance) practices and empowering the next generation through a digital and collaborative approach. Through these localized yet highly strategic steps, we are laying the groundwork for a sustainable movement that benefits the environment, the community, and generations to come.
