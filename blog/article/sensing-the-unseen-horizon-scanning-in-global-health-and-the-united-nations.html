Sensing the Unseen: Horizon Scanning in Global Health and the United Nations

Angga Conni Saputra
•
Apr 12, 2026

In an increasingly interconnected world, crises no longer respect borders. A localized viral spillover, a subtle shift in ocean temperatures, or an emerging dual-use technology can rapidly cascade into a global emergency. To navigate this extreme complexity, relying on historical data is a recipe for strategic failure.

This is where Horizon Scanning transitions from a corporate strategy tool into a critical mechanism for planetary survival. Today, international bodies, particularly the United Nations (UN) and global health agencies, rely on horizon scanning to detect "weak signals" before they escalate into global catastrophes.

Core Definition

Global Horizon Scanning is the systematic exploration of the open environment to identify potential threats, emerging technologies, and systemic shifts early enough to allow for policy intervention, rather than just crisis response.

Horizon Scanning at the United Nations

The United Nations has formally recognized that traditional, reactive governance is insufficient. In the Secretary-General's landmark report, Our Common Agenda, strategic foresight and horizon scanning were mandated as core competencies for the UN 2.0 transformation.

Different UN agencies apply this methodology to varying domains:

  • UNDP (UN Development Programme): Uses scanning to map out emerging socio-economic vulnerabilities, guiding investment into sustainable development before poverty cycles deepen.
  • UNEP (UN Environment Programme): Publishes the "Frontiers" report, which uses environmental horizon scanning to identify emerging issues like urban noise pollution, shifting wildfire patterns, and zoonotic diseases.
  • WHO (World Health Organization): Integrates scanning into its Epidemic Intelligence networks to detect unusual health events from unstructured data sources worldwide.

"The future cannot be predicted, but it can be designed. Horizon scanning is the radar we use to ensure we are inventing a future we actually want to live in, rather than merely reacting to the one we inherit."

Case Study: Global Health and Antimicrobial Resistance (AMR)

One of the most critical applications of Horizon Scanning is in public health and Health Technology Assessment (HTA). Instead of waiting for a disease to spread, researchers scan scientific literature, social media, and demographic data for early warnings.

A profound example is the anticipation of Antimicrobial Resistance (AMR). Long before it became a headline crisis, horizon scanning systems detected increasing mentions of drug-resistant pathogens in local clinical reports.

According to research indexed in PubMed (Horizon scanning for new and emerging health technologies), horizon scanning frameworks are systematically used by national health services to evaluate the clinical and economic impact of new pharmaceuticals before they enter the market. Similarly, the detection of zoonotic spillovers (viruses jumping from animals to humans) relies heavily on scanning unstructured veterinary and ecological data (see related epidemiological foresight studies on PubMed).

Traditional Surveillance vs. Horizon Scanning

To understand why organizations like the UN are shifting towards this methodology, we must look at how it differs from traditional surveillance mechanisms.

FeatureTraditional SurveillanceHorizon Scanning
Primary FocusKnown risks and historical dataUnknowns and emerging "weak signals"
Data SourcesStructured data (e.g., hospital records, census)Unstructured data (e.g., open web, social media, gray literature)
ObjectiveMeasure and monitor current stateAnticipate and shape future state
Response TimeReactive (after an event occurs)Proactive (months or years before impact)

Other Strategic Functions of Horizon Scanning

Beyond global health and international diplomacy, the methodology serves several other critical functions:

1. Dual-Use Technology Assessment:
Monitoring advancements in AI and synthetic biology. Scanning helps policymakers identify when a benign technology might be repurposed for malicious use, allowing for preemptive regulation.

2. Supply Chain Resilience:
Governments use scanning to detect early signs of critical mineral shortages or geopolitical shifts that could disrupt the production of semiconductors and medical supplies.

3. Climate Adaptation Strategy:
Identifying highly localized climate innovations (as seen in grassroots systems) and assessing whether they can be scaled globally to mitigate the impacts of extreme weather events.

Conclusion: Institutionalizing Foresight

Horizon scanning is no longer a luxury for futuristic think-tanks; it is an operational necessity. The adoption of these systems by the UN and global health authorities proves that the cost of being caught off-guard far outweighs the investment required to look ahead.

We can no longer afford to learn from our mistakes after the fact. By integrating horizon scanning into policy-making, we shift from a paradigm of crisis management to one of anticipatory governance.
