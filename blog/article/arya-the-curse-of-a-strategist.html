Arya: The Curse of a Strategist

Angga Conni Saputra
•
August 14, 2026

Some people are gifted talkers. Others are gifted workers. But Arya had something far more troublesome. He was gifted at seeing where things were headed.

Since childhood, Arya always felt his mind worked a little differently. When other people saw an event, he saw the chain of causes behind it. When other people saw a decision, he saw dozens of consequences that might surface months, even years later. To him, the world wasn't a collection of events. The world was a sequence of causes and effects.

The problem was, an ability like that was rarely treated as a gift. More often, it was treated as a nuisance.

Part I — The Curse of a Strategist

Arya knew exactly how gray the territory he operated in really was. Strategy wasn't simply about finding a solution. Strategy meant understanding how people think — what makes them angry, what makes them believe, what makes them decide. And if someone truly understood all of that, they also knew how to bend the direction of another person's decisions.

That was precisely why Arya rarely showed what he was capable of. Not because he couldn't. But because he knew an ability like that was easy to misuse.

There was one more thing that made Arya increasingly careful. For some reason, people tended to believe what he said — even when he was only stating a possibility. A few days later, that possibility often actually happened. Not because Arya could see the future. He simply saw patterns that escaped everyone else's attention. But to the people around him, the difference was almost invisible.

Ironically, there was one person who never truly believed him: his boss. A highly rational Japanese man. Every time Arya tried to explain his reasoning, the man stopped him mid-sentence. Not because he ran out of words to say, but because he had already heard the same kind of explanation too many times before, and it had never once changed his mind.

In the past, Arya had tried explaining how he arrived at a conclusion. The result was always the same. “Hallucinating.” “Too much imagination.” “Nonsense.” “Just a coincidence.” “Lucky again, huh.”

At first, Arya tried to defend himself. Then he tried showing data. Then he tried explaining step by step. The more he explained, the stranger he sounded. Eventually he learned one thing: not every thought process can be translated into words. People only see the final result. They see the accurate prediction, the decision that worked out, the problem that was successfully avoided. And then they say, “Arya's just lucky.”

Arya only smiled. Because he knew luck couldn't possibly show up dozens of times in a row. And if something keeps happening again and again, maybe it isn't luck anymore. Maybe it's a pattern no one else has learned to see yet.

“People often call it luck when they simply can't see the process behind a decision.”

Part II — The Wrong Face for a Warning

Arya was thirty-five years old. Yet almost every time he met someone new, the same questions surfaced. “Still in college?” “Just graduated?” “Intern?”

At first he found it funny. Over time, he realized a young face wasn't only about appearance. It also changed how people weighed an opinion. Arya never minded when people doubted his age. What wore him down was when they started doubting his mind along with it.

For some people, experience has a face. The older someone looks, the easier their words are to trust. The younger someone's face is, the more likely their ideas get dismissed as mere guesses.

One day, Arya noticed something that unsettled him. Not a mistake. A pattern — a sequence of decisions that felt too neat, too convenient, as if someone were quietly steering everyone toward a conclusion that had already been prepared from the start.

He tried to explain it. But before he could finish, a few people simply exchanged glances. “That's overthinking it.” “Probably just a coincidence.” “You've read too much theory.”

Arya recognized that look. The same look from years ago. The look that said, “This kid imagines too much.”

For a moment, Arya almost gave up. He knew the more he explained, the less likely anyone would believe him — because there was something stronger than logic at work. Its name was perception. And perception had already decided he wasn't old enough to understand a game this large.

That night, Arya sat alone. Not thinking about how to convince people. Thinking about how to prove something without needing to ask anyone to believe him first. Then he remembered a principle he'd once learned: if someone doesn't believe a system can be manipulated, don't argue — show them how the manipulation works.

A few days later, Arya began a small experiment. Not to trap anyone. Not to embarrass anyone. He simply wanted to know whether the pattern he'd noticed was actually real. To a few colleagues, he passed along slightly different pieces of information — nothing important, just small details, deliberately altered. Then he waited.

Days went by. And just as he suspected, the stories came back to him — in almost the same shape, through nearly the exact paths he had imagined.

That was when Arya finally stood up and said quietly, “What you just saw was only a simulation.”

The room went silent.

“If information this simple can be steered to exactly where I predicted, imagine what someone could do if they genuinely intended to sabotage a project.”

No one answered. Not because they'd finally started believing Arya. But because, for the first time, they had seen with their own eyes how a perception could be shaped.

Arya didn't feel like he'd won that day. Quite the opposite — he felt sad. Because once again he'd had to prove something in a way he never actually wanted to. He hoped that one day a good argument would simply be judged on its own merit, not on the face of the person delivering it.

“Prejudice doesn't always come from hatred. Sometimes it simply comes from a face judged too young to carry a truth.”

Part III — The Mirror That Never Lies

Arya had already seen the pattern. He knew someone was playing with perception — not through lies, but by letting every single person draw their own conclusion. The problem was: how do you explain something the victim doesn't even realize is happening to them?

Arya had tried before. He drew out the sequence. Explained cause and effect. Pointed to the small inconsistencies that, when strung together, formed a much bigger picture. But the result was always the same. “Arya has too many theories.” “Just a coincidence.” “You're too suspicious.”

He smiled bitterly — not because they were wrong, but because he realized some things simply can't be explained with words. They have to be experienced.

A few days later, Arya came to the office without a presentation. No data. No charts. He carried only a single sheet of paper. On it were a few short sentences. No names. No stated purpose. No accusations. The sentences felt strange, almost as if written specifically for whoever happened to read them.

Arya asked a few colleagues to read it. Then he simply asked, “What do you think?” Nothing more.

The first one smiled. “This is like describing myself.” The second nodded slowly. “Strange... it feels like the writer knows me.” The third fell silent for quite a while. “I don't know why, but this feels very personal.”

Arya only took notes. No comments. A few minutes later, he collected the sheets back and laid them side by side on the table. The room suddenly went quiet. Because everyone had just realized they'd read the exact same sentences. Not a single word was different. A few people laughed out of embarrassment. Others exchanged confused glances. How could one identical paragraph feel so personal to every single person?

Arya stayed quiet. It wasn't time to explain yet. He simply looked around the room. “Look. I didn't lie to any of you just now. I didn't force anyone. I only gave you a mirror. And each of you saw a different person in it.”

The room fell silent again. Arya closed his folder.

“What just happened is the reason someone can control the opinions of many people without ever telling a single lie. And the person we're dealing with right now... is using exactly the same principle.”

Part IV — What I Never Wanted to Show You

The room was still silent. A few people were still staring at the sheet of paper they had just read — one paragraph, identical words, yet every one of them had felt it was written specifically for them.

Arya took a long breath. “So... do you understand now why I didn't just explain everything from the start? If I'd said your perception was being played with, you would have called it too much theory. If I'd said someone was exploiting how the human mind works, you probably would have laughed. But now that you've experienced it yourselves, I don't need to ask you to believe me anymore.”

He picked the sheets back up. “You weren't fooled by me. You were fooled by your own minds.”

The room went quiet again. “What just happened has a name.”

The Barnum Effect

A psychological phenomenon where people perceive a very general statement as being remarkably personal and accurate. Research on this effect dates back to Bertram Forer's 1948 experiment, in which almost every participant believed the personality description they received had been written specifically for them — when in reality, every single participant had received the exact same text.

Arya continued. “The sentences didn't know any of you. What knew you... was your own mind.” He wrote two words on the board. Projection. Confirmation.

“And then the mind gets to work. You go looking for a matching experience. You connect it to a memory. You ignore the part that doesn't fit. And eventually... you convince yourself the writing was true.”

Confirmation Bias & Subjective Validation

This is what psychology recognizes as confirmation bias and subjective validation — the tendency of people to look for evidence that supports their own beliefs, then treat something as true simply because it feels personally meaningful. A statement doesn't need to be accurate to be believed. It only needs to feel like it belongs to you.

Arya looked around the room, meeting every face one by one. “If a single paragraph can make us believe something, imagine what someone could do with a news story. With gossip. With a meeting. With social media. With a sentence deliberately left vague. And then every single person will fill in the blank themselves.”

One of his colleagues finally asked, quietly, “So... you turned us into an experiment?”

Arya nodded. “Yes. Not to embarrass you. But because I've learned something. Some lessons can't be taught. They can only be experienced. I promise you, I wished there were another way. I wished explaining alone would have been enough. But every time I tried, I was told I was imagining things. Hallucinating. Lucky. So today, I chose to prove it instead.”

No one interrupted him again. For the first time, everyone listened until the end — not because Arya spoke any louder, but because they had just felt, firsthand, how their own minds could be steered without ever noticing it.

Arya closed his folder. He should have felt relieved. Instead, something else crept in. Unease. One question kept circling in his head: if a simple experiment like this could work this well, how many of the big decisions people made were never really born from logic at all — but from a perception someone else had deliberately built?

He looked out the window and said quietly, “And the person we're dealing with... doesn't just understand psychology. They understand people.”

He was about to say something else when his phone lit up on the table — a message from a number he didn't recognize, sent, according to the timestamp, at the exact minute he'd finished folding his sheets of paper back together. He hadn't told anyone about the experiment beforehand. He hadn't written anything down anywhere it could be read.

Only two lines were visible before the screen locked itself again.

“Nicely demonstrated, Arya. Now show them what happens when the mirror is held up to you.”

Arya's hand hovered over the phone. He hadn't shown anyone that message yet. He wasn't sure, standing there in the sudden quiet of the room, whether he wanted to.

Because for the first time since he'd learned to read patterns other people couldn't see, Arya understood he wasn't the only one in the building who knew how to build a mirror — and he had no idea yet whose reflection was waiting inside the next one.
