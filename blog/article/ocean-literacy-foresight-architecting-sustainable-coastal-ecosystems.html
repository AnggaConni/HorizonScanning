Ocean literacy is not merely about understanding the ocean as a physical space—it is about recognizing it as a complex, interconnected system that directly shapes human life. In Indonesia, the world's largest archipelagic nation, ocean literacy is a strategic foundation for environmental sustainability and long-term societal resilience.

Indonesia’s coastal regions play a critical role in maintaining the balance between terrestrial and marine ecosystems. However, in recent decades, these areas have faced increasing environmental pressures: marine debris pollution, declining mangrove coverage, and the escalating impacts of climate change. These challenges are not only ecological—they directly affect the social and economic stability of coastal communities.

What is Ocean Literacy?

Ocean literacy is the understanding of the ocean’s influence on you and your influence on the ocean. It emphasizes that every individual—especially younger generations—has a role in protecting marine ecosystems through informed awareness and meaningful, structured action.

Case Study: Balikpapan Bay

Balikpapan Bay is one of the most important coastal ecosystems in East Kalimantan, particularly due to its extensive mangrove forests. However, land-use change and coastal development have placed increasing pressure on this ecosystem.

Satellite-based studies have shown a decline in mangrove coverage over recent years, highlighting the urgency of data-driven intervention and multi-stakeholder collaboration to preserve ecological balance.

Why Mangrove Ecosystems Matter

  • Act as natural coastal protection against extreme waves, storms, and erosion.
  • Store massive amounts of carbon efficiently (known as blue carbon ecosystems).
  • Provide critical habitat for biodiversity, including the endemic proboscis monkey (Nasalis larvatus).

Mangroves are not just coastal vegetation—they are natural infrastructure that supports climate resilience and ecological stability.

From Signals to Evidence: Bayesian Thinking

Environmental decision-making often suffers from deep uncertainty. For example: is a sudden mangrove degradation a temporary seasonal fluctuation, or is it the start of a long-term collapsing trend?

By applying Bayesian reasoning, we can continuously update our confidence level as new field data becomes available—transforming gut assumptions into hard, evidence-based insight.

Strategic Decisions: Knowing When to Act

In environmental governance, inaction is often more costly than action. However, interventions must be strategic, negotiated, and well-timed.

Applying the BATNA (Best Alternative to a Negotiated Agreement) concept helps policymakers, NGOs, and corporate partners evaluate the true long-term cost of environmental inaction versus the cost of intervention.

From Knowledge to Behavior: The COM-B Framework

Ocean literacy ultimately depends on behavior change. Knowledge and awareness alone are never enough to save an ecosystem.

  • Capability: Do the students and communities physically and psychologically understand how to protect coastal ecosystems?
  • Opportunity: Do they have the physical access, tools, and social support to participate in environmental action?
  • Motivation: Are they driven by intrinsic values or external incentives to protect their environment?

Programs like the Mangrove Action Lab & Coastal Challenge demonstrate how experiential learning can activate all three components—turning passive students into active environmental stewards.

Ocean literacy is not just about understanding the ocean—it is about redesigning how we interact with it.

By combining environmental awareness with structured intelligence frameworks (Horizon Scanning, Bayesian Logic, Systems Mapping, BATNA, and COM-B), we move from passive knowledge to actionable change.

Because the future of coastal ecosystems will not be determined by what we know—but by what we choose to do next.
